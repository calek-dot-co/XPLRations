Toxic Mining Lakes

Somewhere, Cyprus

Blue lake

Glowing bright blue, the result of copper sulphates and light scattering through murky water, this is one of three toxic mining lakes I hiked to during my visit to Cyprus.

This lake partly fills a small mine that operated briefly at this location between 1971 and 1972. While it has sat dormant since then, that may not be the case forever. If future copper prices rise enough, there is a small chance that this mine could reopen. It remains one of the last economically viable deposits in Cyprus where copper, iron, and gold dust are still present.

Red lake

The next lake I trekked to appeared even more toxic. Shifting between deep red and a luminous orange depending on the angle, this large body of water is heavily stained by iron oxides from mining tailings.

Tailings represent the industrial "leftovers"—the waste material discarded once all minerals of value have been extracted. This slurry of crushed rock, water, and residual processing chemicals is contained in these basins to settle and evaporate. As long as you don't go swimming, these lakes are pretty harmless. You just have to ignore the risk of tailings leaching into the groundwater, the risk of dam failures, or the risk of toxic dust being picked up by the wind and spread across the island.

Green lake

The final lake I visited could almost pass for a healthy, algae-filled lake. However, that illusion breaks when you notice the mineral rich layer of dirt that rises out of the depths to meet the dead grasses. While the green hue likely stems from a similar chemical makeup to the first lake, subtle shifts in copper sulphate concentrations yield a luminescent green at this location. Fitting for an island which gets its name from the Greek word for copper, kypros.

As I left the final lake, I wondered: how do does Cyprus even go about cleaning these messes up? And who will pay for it? It sure won't be the mining companies that took their profits from the land until they were no longer in business. It's just another mess left to the future generations to solve, by past generations who didn't know any better.
