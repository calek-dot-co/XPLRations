Armenian Alphabet Monument

Artashavan, Armenia

Designed in 405 AD by Mesrop Mashtots, the Armenian Alphabet was created to translate the Bible into Armenian, a fitting reason to create an alphabet for the world's first Christian nation. Drawing partial inspiration from the Greek alphabet, it originally comprised 36 letters, with three more added during the Middle Ages, bringing the total to 39.

To commemorate the 1600th anniversary of the alphabet’s creation, this monument was unveiled in 2005. Each letter is carved from tuff, a volcanic stone native to Armenia, celebrated for its rich hues of pink, orange, and red.

Displayed below are photographs of 38 of the 39 Armenian letters. Interestingly, the character 'և' is absent from the monument. Its omission reflects ongoing debate about whether it should be recognised as a standalone letter or simply a ligature combination of 'Ե' and 'Լ'.
