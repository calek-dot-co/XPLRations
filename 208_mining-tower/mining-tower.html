Rusting Mining Tower

Somewhere, Cyprus

In the foothills of Cyprus lie the remains of a large mining complex. The site consists of a few mine shafts, a very red lake, and the reason for my visit: the old head frame of a vertical mine shaft.

This mine operated from 1954 to 1979 and during its lifetime approximately 475,000 tonnes of ore were extracted from the depths of this mountain. Some say copper was mined here, others say specifically pyrite from the shaft, and from the nearby open-cast mine iron and sulfur. From what I saw, the vibrant yellow earth suggests that it was copper and sulfur.

“The mine... where man, son of wind and sun, crawls like a worm in a hopeless search for the door that will breathe life into him... The mine, you are both a curse and an accuser, a legend and a story. That around whose dark existence, theater and cinema, novel and song have woven their webs.”

– Kostas Montis

The rusting yellow three-storey headframe was once used to pull cart after cart out of the ground. It achieved this with the help of the accompanying building, which contained a generator and a large motor connected to a spool of thick wire. Two wires left the building and rose to the top of the tower, meeting the large steel wheels on the third floor. This combination lowered and lifted carts and miners in and out of the mines below.

As one load went up, another one went down into the depths. From what I could find online, this vertical shaft reaches a depth of 134 metres, connecting to tunnels “336” and “400” at 64 meter intervals.

Somewhere in these depths it is said that they also found “Devil's Mud”, a mixture of materials with roughly 106g of gold and 690g of silver per tonne. While this specific shaft remained inaccessible during my visit, the conclusion of this exploration offers a glimpse into a nearby ventilation tunnel.

The four round structures shown above and below are known as tipples. In this mechanical sequence, an ore cart travels into the middle and stops, the tipple and cart then rotate 180° together, which dumps the ore into the hoppers below.

Once inverted and empty, they both rotate back to upright, and the cart moves onwards. This cycle repeats continuously, with the raw material being then transported by truck to nearby processing facilities.

Above, a maze of steel winds in efficient loops, transporting ore away from the mines. Below, the toxic remains of decades of this loop's work. Its a landscape in contrast between human intervention and the natural world.

Even on a very cloudy day, the waste earth excavated alongside the valuable ores leaves a bright stain on the landscape. These piles of yellow dirt, eroded by rain, bleed into nearby rivers where the tailings collect in bright pools.

Exploring one of the mining tunnels

At one point in time this tunnel was also linked via narrow-gauge rail to the tower, but the track is now long gone. Through the open gate into the darkness you are first greeted by a steel-framed section of tunnel “464”, which still looks in great condition today.

Shortly after that the tunnel gets a bit rougher, from small collapses to a small metal door that is bent open from the top just enough for a person to squeeze through.

After that is a short fully concrete section before more traditional tunnel sections with wooden frames.

Past sections of solid rock, I eventually got to a section with many collapsed wooden beams and decided not to push my luck any further. It was the tower that was the main attraction for this visit anyway. I made my way back quickly and was quite glad to be back surrounded again by daylight.
