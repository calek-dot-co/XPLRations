Object 3001

Podborsko, Poland

During the Cold War, the spread of nuclear deterrence in the 1960s and beyond significantly reshaped the landscapes of both the East and the West. Hidden in forests, buried deep underground, and secured with heavy fortifications.

Within the Eastern Bloc, the Soviet Army was the only Warsaw Pact force permitted to possess nuclear warheads. However, the strategic need for rapid-response nuclear weapons positioned closer to Western Europe than mainland Russia led to the formation of secret agreements. These agreements allowed other Warsaw Pact nations to host Soviet nuclear weapons in purpose built bunkers.

One notable type of storage facility, known as a “Monolith” due to its massive, block-like design, consisted of paired semi-subterranean bunkers. Each was constructed with enormous reinforced doors, oriented in different directions to better withstand blast waves and protect the up to 180 nuclear warheads per bunker.

Object 3001 is one of three Monolith bunkers built in Poland and remains the most well-preserved of its kind in Eastern Europe. Today it operates as a museum, offering guided tours in Polish every Sunday. On this tour you get a glimpse into the past and a future that never happened.
