The Pink City

Yerevan, Armenia

Over the centuries, Yerevan has been known by many names—Erebuni, Erevani, Erevan, and ultimately Yerevan (Armenian: Երևան, Russian: Ереван)—yet its core meaning has remained unchanged: 'Ere' meaning "first", and 'buni/van' meaning "place" or "site". Today, it is also informally known as “The Pink City”, a reference to the distinctive pink tuff stone used in the construction of much of its architecture.

Location: 40.253953, 44.589614

Tuff stone, formed from the compacted ash and pumice of volcanic eruptions and quarried from the Armenian Highlands, is lightweight, workable, and naturally porous—making it excellent for insulation. Nearly all of Yerevan’s buildings use this material, there wasn't a single all glass skyscraper in sight. While best known for its iconic pink-orange hue, Armenian tuff comes in 27 other shades including purple, red, orange, brown, and black.

Located just off the Republic Square metro station stands the Flower Fountain, a Soviet-era masterpiece crafted from concrete and tuff. Even when inactive, it is worth a visit.

Location: 40.178642, 44.516008

The Yeritasardakan metro station, recognisable by its tube-like form extending from the roof, is a visually compelling contrast between its corrugated, cylindrical extension and boxy first floor building, is not just aesthetic—it is the extension of the diagonal escalator shaft that travels from street level down 37 metres to the train platforms.

Location: 40.187274, 44.521371

The Cascade Complex is both a monumental staircase and a cultural hub, housing a museum and offering a popular social space for both locals and tourists. Yet even this impressive structure is overshadowed by what lies beyond it on a clear day: a breathtaking view of the pink city and Mt Ararat.
