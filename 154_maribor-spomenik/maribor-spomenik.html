Liberation Monument

Maribor, Slovenia

This giant brass spomenik rises from the earth in a central square of Maribor. Re-emerging, much as the local Slovene culture would have after the German occupation during World War II.

Before the invasion of Yugoslavia by Axis forces in 1941, Maribor was a town very close to the expanding German Reich border. After the invasion that April, Maribor was a town occupied by the German Reich and subject to the Nazi's Germanisation through oppression and ethnic cleansing.

This was met with resistance from the local Partisans and the Liberation Front, who, despite their much fewer numbers, provided formidable adversaries through use of guerrilla tactics. In response, the Germans started taking innocent local Maribor Slovenes as hostages, executing batches of them after each resistance attack. Roughly 700 hostages were executed in total before the war ended.

The nearly 200 faces shown etched into the brass, illustrated in a halftone-line style, have been taken directly from the archives of the Museum of the National Liberation in Maribor. They are the faces of the Yugoslav war heroes who gave their lives fighting against fascism.
