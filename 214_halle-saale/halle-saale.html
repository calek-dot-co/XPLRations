Tile Murals of the GDR

Halle (Saale), Germany

Halle (Saale) and Halle-Neustadt are home to many pieces of Soviet-era artworks on buildings. While some of the local Plattenbau (pre-fabricated) buildings and fully abandoned, many remain inhabited. Among them is a former educational centre turned immigration office, which features two striking twelve-story tile murals. These works depict traditional Eastern Bloc motifs, celebrating both eastern philosophy and scientific progress.

The forces of nature and technology mastered by humans

On the left staircase of this building is a tile mural that depicts space, industry, and the power of work. While it may have been important work for the Soviet Union, Renau’s portrayal of the labourers at the base of the composition and cooler colour theme conveys to me a sense of despondency, rather than triumph.

By José Renau, 1974. Location: 51.481666, 11.914704

Working class unity and founding of the GDR

On the right staircase of this building is a tile mural that depicts Eastern philosopher Karl Marx, agriculture, and the power of solidarity. The colour, overall style, and flow from top to bottom make this one my favourite of the two murals.

By José Renau, 1974. Location: 51.482215, 11.914713

He stirred the sleep of the world

A different medium was used for this mural, silicate painting on cement tiles. It presents Lenin alongside the Soviet Union's pillars of reform: elimination of illiteracy, land redistribution, and rollout of electrification.

By Erich Enge, 1971. Location: 51.474821, 11.914385

The peaceful use of nuclear energy

Another great tile mural by Renau, this one on the other side of Halle. It depicts the 1960s/70s East German propaganda goal of utilising nuclear power to move away from brown coal for energy production. The artwork illustrates this with two hands reaching toward the sun, flanked on both sides by parabolic mirrors.

By José Renau, 1971. Location: 51.480721, 11.981533

Untitled (swimming pool mural)

This piece offers a contemporary perspective on historical motifs, depicting three figures in a scene that evokes the traditional East German Freikörperkultur (FKK). The mural’s subjects are presented in the quintessential GDR style: textile-free and uninhibited.

By Ronald Kobe, 1996. Location: 51.479400, 11.915377
