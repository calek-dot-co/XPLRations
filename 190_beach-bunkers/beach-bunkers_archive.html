Danish Beach Bunkers

Vestkystruten, Denmark

Norre Lyngby Beach

Denmark's north-west coastline features long sandy beaches, rolling grassy dunes, and small coastal towns. Scattered between all of this, you'll also find the huge concrete remains of World War II defences.

Vigsø Beach

Each group of bunkers is distinct, even when constructed from the same sets of standardised designs. Some locations contain only a handful of bunkers, while others feature more than 30. Many remain buried beneath grass and dunes, others are fully exposed along the beaches, or, as seen in Vigsø, are gradually being reclaimed by erosion and the sea.

Klitmøller Beach

Originally built to defend against an Allied invasion of Europe, the Atlantic Wall is a line of World War II bunkers built by Nazi Germany between 1942 and 1945, by the German Army primarily using forced labourers.

Lyngby Beach

Each location originally started as sandy trenches and wooden shacks, but was slowly upgraded to huge concrete bunkers with walls and ceilings up to 3.5 metres thick. These sites were positioned at key defensive points along Denmark’s coast.

Agger Beach

Many bunkers once featured gun emplacements or were simple structures for shelter, but this specific tall bunker at Agger was once used to hold a radar dish. It was one of many radar stations networked together to cover all of Denmark's airspace. But no matter the original purpose, they are all in the same degraded state today.

Thyborøn Beach

As Denmark was the first country on the western coast to fall under German control, the bunkers in this region were among the earliest to be constructed and are also the most numerous. The Atlantic Wall also extended well beyond Denmark to include France, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Norway, where similar bunkers can still be found today.

Spidsbjerg Beach

The alternative to leaving these bunkers exposed to the elements has been to blow them up, although this approach was applied only to several hundred bunkers located in or near cities. It is estimated that around 7,000 bunkers still remain along Denmark’s coastline today.

Nymindegab Beach

While most beachfront bunkers are empty or partially filled with sand, rare exceptions remain sealed decades after World War II. One such bunker, recently opened, contained a wide range of rusted hardware and discarded materials.

Blåvand Beach

While many coastal bunkers are gradually being reclaimed by the sea, others in more popular locations have been given a new lease of life through art. Illuminated by the nearby lighthouse, artist Bill Woodrow’s work, "Mules", symbolises the evils of the past being dragged out to sea.
