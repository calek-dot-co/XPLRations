The Abandoned City

Varosha, Cyrpus

Once celebrated as the “Las Vegas of the Mediterranean,” Varosha served as the centre of Cypriot tourism throughout the 1960s and early 1970s. Its landscape, comprised over 6,000 plots of land, supported a ecosystem of local residents, international tourists, businesses, and high-rise hotels.

This beachside city, which previously accounted for 45% of the island’s total hotel capacity, was transformed almost overnight when Cyprus was partitioned during the 1974 occupation of the north. Today, much of Varosha remains frozen in time, half preserved and half decaying.

Close to the Green Line UN border zone, but not part of it, Varosha is a restricted area now controlled by the Turkish military. Its been kept empty, with vacant buildings, overgrown streets, and decaying infrastructure, with the hope that it would one day eventually be traded back to the Greek Cypriots in exchange for political concessions. No deal has been reached so far.

As time progresses, little changes, except for little changes. Since the 6th of October 2020, Varosha has very slowly been reopening to the public, mostly as a tourist walking route between the abandoned buildings.

Signs like the one above, along with many cameras, aim to discourage visitors from straying from the tourist paths and into houses and businesses owned by Greek and Turkish Cypriots who have been unable to return home since the evacuation.

Undeveloped for over five decades, the architecture of Varosha is straight out of the 50s, 60s, and 70s. From Art Deco-inspired buildings with their curved sunrooms, to what were then cutting-edge, floor-to-ceiling glass storefronts.

This range of styles, sometimes collectively referred to as Mediterranean Modernism, was from an era of rapid growth and flashy tourism. It remains a monument to a period of growth that ended with a sudden and (so far) permanent halt.

It wouldn't be Northern Cyprus without two very large flags casting a shadow somewhere over the land.

Here in the north, suspended from the former schoolhouse, are the mostly red Turkish national flag and the mostly white flag of the Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus (TRNC). They serve as constant reminder of the region's current political identity.

Wandering down the few streets open to tourists, you'll pass a large variety of scenery. Small overgrown roads transition to wide streets, small houses are overshadowed by large abandoned hotels, and as you get closer to the seafront, piles of rubble give way to the amazing mediterranean ocean horizon.

When I ended my exploration, I was far from where I started. My loop had taken me from one end of the beach, through the centre of Varosha, to the other end of the beach, leaving me to contemplate the future of this abandoned part of Cyprus.

Ersin Tatar, the prime minister of Northern Cyprus, recently stated that "our aim is to demolish some buildings and restore others, bringing them into the national economy ... the opening of Varosha under our sovereignty has served as a major contribution to the Turkish world."

This raises a question of identity for the region. Does Varosha belong to the Turkish world? Or to an independent Cyprus? Or to Greece through enosis? The answers to those questions depends on who you ask.

As Varosha slowly starts to reopen, street by street, building by building, we will have to wait and observe as the process unfolds.
