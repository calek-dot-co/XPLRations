Freedom Hill Monument

Dobrljin, Bosnia and Herzegovina

Situated not far from the Croatian border, but somehow also in the middle of nowhere in Bosnia & Herzegovina, on the top of a rural hill, is a large stone flower spomenik. No, not that stone flower spomenik in Jasenovac, this one is a little different. More brutalistic in design. Smaller in scale, but also somehow heavier looking. This is the Gligino Freedom Hill Monument.

During the invasion and occupation of the Kingdom of Yugoslavia by Axis forces in April 1941, the Ustaše (Croatia's nationalist movement), saw an opportunity to create an ethnically pure Croat state. Through persecution, violence, and murder, non-Croat ethnicities like Serbs, Jews, and Roma were oppressed. In response, many of these oppressed groups united to form local resistance movements, often known collectively as the Partisans.

This spomenik's design represents that resistance, the local people who gave their lives, and the eventual victory. The hard concrete shapes of a blooming flower are a metaphor for rebirth and renewal in post–World War II Yugoslavia. Yet, progress should never be seen as guaranteed. From the decades of Tito’s authoritarian rule to the recent pro-Russian graffiti defacing the memorial, these are stark reminders of the ongoing struggles still faced across Europe today.
