Monument Park Knjaževac

Andrijevica, Montenegro

Control of Andrijevica in eastern Montenegro was heavily contested throughout World War II. The town and valley shifted hands more than 15 times between the Italians, the Partisans, and the Germans, resulting in the deaths of hundreds of Partisan fighters and over a thousand civilians.

To remember those who fought and died for their homeland, this spomenik was unveiled in 1967 on the 26th anniversary of the town's final liberation from Axis forces.

At first glance it's easy to assume this is just another large concrete spomenik, but actually it is composed of huge marble blocks stacked one on top of the other, twenty-two metres high. Symbolically, each of the monument’s six pillars is said to represent one of the former socialist republics of Yugoslavia, all converging at the central eternal flame that signifies the unity and spirit of the Yugoslav people.

→ More info at the Spomenik Database

Translation front (above):
"During the Liberation War, from July 13, 1941, to May 15, 1945, for the freedom, brotherhood, and unity of our peoples, 608 fighters from the Andrijevica district fell. In the same period, 857 inhabitants of this region lost their lives due to fascist terror."

Translation top (not shown):
"They gave everything they had for the freedom of the people and the honor of their homeland; let us repay them with unity in prosperity and by following their example in hardship."

From the right vantage point, you can get a closer look at the now-smashed eternal flame. Made from red glass, it sits atop the junction of the six pillars and represents that the memory of the village's sacrifice would always burn bright for the people of Yugoslavia.
