Object 1180

Somewhere, Moldova

In a remote forest somewhere in Moldova, rubble and rust welcome you into the past. But not just any past, a specific version of the past driven by immense paranoia and preparation unlike anything seen before.

This is Object 1180, a Soviet-era monster designed to protect the Moldovan Socialist Soviet Republic government and military officials in the event of nuclear war.

Constructed from concrete-filled steel forms, the sheer scale of this bunker is almost unimaginable in size.

The structure is made from two cylindrical excavations, each 36 metres in diameter and 60 metres deep. Each hole is filled with a 13 floor bunker, completely concealed underground. The bunkers and the outside facilities are linked with a maze of tunnels that would have provided access, power, and supplies. Each underground tower is capped with a two-metre-thick roof designed to withstand conventional weaponry.

To ensure horizontal isolation, each bunker is designed to be slightly smaller than its containing excavation. Hydraulic dampers integrated into the lowest floor also provide a shock-absorbing buffer vertically.

This double isolation ensures the structures remains protected against the seismic shocks generated by nuclear detonations.

Gaining access to inside the southernmost bunker proved surprisingly straightforward, requiring only a little bit of climbing. Due to partial deconstruction, the space between the bunker and the excavation is now exposed, resulting in significant gap in the exterior walls.

Each of the two bunkers was designed with a specific purpose in mind. When connected, they created a completely self-sufficient fallout shelter.

One bunker was the main living quarters, complete with control room, offices, a medical centre, bedrooms, and even a morgue. The other bunker contained all of the support systems: diesel power generators, fuel tanks, air purification, workshops, and warehouses.

Only a single staircase remains intact within the entire complex; all others have rusted and collapsed. For those able to navigate the dark, maze-like floor plans, this provides a precarious route to the lower levels.

While one flight was likely removed to restrict access, a previous explorer fortunately installed a sturdy replacement, restoring access to the floors below.

From the third level downwards, the cold of winter can be found trapped within the hallways and rooms of this bunker. Water that once dripped from the roof is now frozen in stalactites. Frost covers the walls, and a layer of ice makes navigating from room to room difficult.

If you look down into the void between the bunker and the outer structure, you'll see a layer of snow on top of frozen groundwater flooding. During construction the groundwater was kept out by actively freezing the surrounding soil, a process that obviously stopped long ago, allowing nature to partially reclaim the structure.

Construction on this bunker began in 1985. It was to be part of a network of seven Supreme Command facilities commissioned for the south-western Soviet front. Development only ever commenced on four of the seven planned sites due to the collapse of the USSR in 1991.

Object 1161 in Belarus represents the second most complete counterpart bunker. It has now been covered in a thick layer of dirt, but is rumoured to be accessible if you know the right entrance.

Two additional unnumbered facilities in Azerbaijan and Ukraine were also commissioned, however, from what I could find online their construction was abandoned during the initial excavation phase.

This bunker was apparently 95% complete when it was abandoned. The main structure was complete and most services were hooked up. All it needed was furniture and supplies.

However, Moldova’s transition to democracy prompted a freeze on spending required for such monolithic projects. This bunker was left unfinished, unguarded from looters, vulnerable to scrappers, and slowly rusting.

As I continued deeper into the structure, I arrived on the sixth of thirteen floors. Here is where the groundwater level meets you and a solid layer of ice blocks your path. On this final accessible floor, a thin layer of water covers ice thick enough to stand on.

It is rumoured that even deeper underground are the remains of a train tunnel that links to the capital for the safe extraction and transfer of VIPs, avoiding contact with the surface. This is likely just a rumour, but until this pit is drained, we can't be sure.

As scrappers slowly continue to chip away at the ground-level steel, lower levels are reclaimed by rising groundwater, and brilliant orange rust develops on every wall... this bunker's future remains unknown. Often called the poorest country in Europe, Moldova has many more important things to invest in than the removal or restoration of this wonderful disaster.
