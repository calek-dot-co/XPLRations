Šušnjar Memorial Complex

Sanski Most, Bosnia and Herzegovina

Rising from the centre of an overgrown cemetery is a flickering flame made from curved aluminium panels. Designed to symbolise the light of life, it serves as an inclusive symbol representing all the region’s diverse religious and ethnic communities who lost lives during World War II.

Before the Axis invasion of the Bosanska Krajina region during World War II, Serbs, Croats, Bosniaks, and Jews lived side by side in Sanski Most. In the summer of 1941, however, during the Axis occupation, between 2,000 and 10,000 ethnic Serb and Jewish civilians from the surrounding area were deported to Jasenovac or executed at the Šušnjar killing fields.

It’s on the Šušnjar field that this spomenik and cemetery were built. From the nearby road, visitors first encounter rows of gravestones that lead along overgrown paths to a stone courtyard framed by large concrete beams. A crypt, memorial walls, and sculptures can also be found within the complex.

Following the conflicts of the 1990s, the site fell into neglect, its pathways and structures left to deteriorate. Yet each year, on August 2nd, locals gather here in remembrance of those who were lost.
