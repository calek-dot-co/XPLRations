Iron Fountain

Gyumri, Armenia

At 11:41 AM on December 7th, 1988, a 6.8 magnitude earthquake struck northern Armenia. The cities of Spitak, Vanadzor (formerly Kirovakan), and Gyumri (formerly Leninakan) were the hardest hit, resulting in the deaths of over 25,000 people and over 130,000 injuries. Investigations later revealed that substandard construction during the Brezhnevian Stagnation era (1964–1984) significantly contributed to the scale of the devastation and loss of life.

While many of these areas have rebuilt over the past three decades, some have not. The Iron Fountain is one place that has yet to regain its former glory. Previously a grand meeting spot, full of people and flowing water, today it is a rusted monument surrounded by potholes and small shacks. Yet even in neglect, it retains a certain character—evocative of Soviet-era design, with its distinctive use of materials, cultural motifs, and ideological symbolism.

During my visit to the fountain, I encountered a Ukrainian-Armenian grandmother, someone I suspect many visitors come across. Thanks to a translation app, we shared a long conversation about the area, the earthquake, and life in eastern Europe. She showed me photographs taken before the disaster, images that captured the Iron Fountain at the height of its beauty.
