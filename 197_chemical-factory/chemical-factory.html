Abandoned Chemical Plant

Somewhere, Greece

Early on a cold winter's morning, as I crested a small hill, a great view unfolded below me. In the valley ahead lay the remnants of an old chemical plant. Once powered by lignite from nearby open-cut mines, this vast industrial complex was built to produce ammonium nitrate fertiliser.

Operations ceased in 1997, driven by high running costs, outdated equipment, and competition from imports. Now, it stands largely abandoned—another scar etched into a Greek landscape already marked by mining.

Here are a some photos from my visit, where I attempted to follow the rusted production lines from beginning to end.

Lignite unloading

From my hilltop vantage point, it was a short walk and scramble down to the plant’s railway terminus. As I balanced along the wooden sleepers, I entered through the same path where coal once arrived on site.

Lignite is the lowest grade of coal. Brown, soft, and half between rock and peat. It is known for its high moisture content and low energy yield. In the past, it would have been delivered here and dumped onto the steel grates flanking either side of the tracks, then crushed to size, and transferred onto conveyor belts that distributed it throughout the facility.

Preparation building

Connecting one building to another, I followed the path of a long rubber conveyor belt that transported the lignite to the preparation lines. This seven-storey building was a maze of pipes, hoppers, railings, and staircases.

Control room 1

Hidden in the middle of this large building are the first control rooms I found, each with one or more large green control panels. As far as I can find out, this room controlled the drying and milling of lignite. Each button, knob, graph, and gauge once controlled air flow, gas flow, hoppers, mills, and cyclones. Everything here was manually supervised, not computer-controlled like modern plants. Together, these systems prepared the lignite to the precise moisture level required for gasification.

Although I can’t say for certain, it appears that two or three parallel process lines were controlled from this group of rooms.

Each main wall featured one of three distinct green control panels. Some panels are clearly labelled with the manufacturer’s name—Koppers Essen (above), while others (below) appear slightly more modern, suggesting the plant may have been expanded over time.

Gasification building

This next building houses the heart of the location, the gasification and chemical-processing equipment that once produced ammonia nitrate fertiliser. The main hall holds many huge, multi-part, multi-storey bulbous tanks, covered in decaying insulation and rusted inspection hatches.

Translation: “It is strictly forbidden to use the firefighting equipment for any purpose other than extinguishing a fire.”

Control room 2

Down a narrow hallway off the gasification hall lies the second green control room. It was designed and manufactured by Heinrich Koppers G.m.b.H. Essen, one of Germany’s leading industrial engineering firms specialising in coal and lignite gasification during the 20th century.

Reactor temperatures, gas pressures, and flow rates were all managed from this long control room, lined with hundrets of switches, graphs, and handles. The control wall features six distinct sets of controls, each corresponding to one of the individual gasifier lines in the building.

Despite significant water damage in the room, the control panels remain remarkably well preserved, at least from the front. The rear side, not visible in these photos, has been completely gutted, allowing daylight to shine through and backlight the individual switches and read-outs. The backlight effect gives the wall an uncanny sense that the machinery could spring back to life at any moment.

Manager's office

Next door to the second control room, I also found signs of the people who used to work in this place. In its current state, this room is as dirty and dusty as the type of hazardous work everyone would have done here during the facility’s peak.

Sign translation: “After every shutdown of 6 kV motors, at least 15 minutes must pass before they are restarted. Signed: Management"

Storage tanks

Once processed, the nitric and sulfuric acids were likely stored in these large cylindrical tanks. Their floating roofs accommodated fluctuations in volume as material levels changed over time.

These spherical tanks show below likely held the further processed liquid ammonia, before it was converted into agricultural products and readied for distribution.

Water towers

The top of these tall, elevated reservoirs can be reached by what seemed like an endless spiral staircase. Floor after floor, you rise up the interior column until you gain access to two levels of water tanks. The bottom tank was dirty and dark, the top tank was tiled and relatively much cleaner.

Cooling towers

My final stop on this exploration was the pair of massive cooling towers that supported the entire gasification process. These structures air-cooled large volumes of hot water, which collected at their base before being recirculated into the plant. Unlike other cooling towers I’ve visited, I didn’t come across any traces of shellfish once used to help purify the water—only decaying wood remained.

All of the above is probably only half of the whole abandoned industrial complex, and it took most of the day to explore.

With any luck, I'll get another chance to visit this beauty before it's demolished, hopefully seeing the two other control rooms I didn’t get to see on this visit.

Until next time.
