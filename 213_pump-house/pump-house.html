Abandoned Pump House

Somewhere, Germany

Not far from the fjords of Northern Germany, in a small valley beneath the railway lines, lies a lake and an old abandoned pump house. Covered in graffiti from local youths, perforated steel security screens to try and keep them out, and, on the day I visited, the winter's last snow. Entrance was easy through an old window where an earlier visitor had removed the security screen.

This building was once a pump house for the surrounding neighbourhood and railway station, designed to harvest groundwater flowing from a nearby spring. However, by the mid-70s, the area was connected to the municipal city water grid, and the “Bahnwasserwerk” was simply shut down and left to rot.

Excluded from the now over a decade old neighbourhood renovation plan, the future of this building and lake is unknown. The development plan didn't last long, since the local court explicitly cited the “Quellbiotop” (spring biome) as the reason the development project was rules illegal and subsequently cancelled.

The very spring that once gave this building and local area purpose, is now the primary obstacle to its second life.

Pipe room

Under the German standard DIN 2403, pipes must be painted in different colours depending on what they carry. Green always signifies water.

The specific “Kranwasser” label on the black sign could mean this is drinking water; or, given the nearby train station, it could also mean this water was intended for use in steam locomotives via a water crane.

Tank room

It seems the two large tanks shown above and below are for storage of processed ready-to-drink water. Although I doubt it's still drinkable today.

My best guess for this underground tank is that it stores raw water taken directly from the spring, before it is processed.

Blue room

The blue room is the remains of an old industrial water aeration and filtration system. Water would drip down from the metal tubes onto circular, notched ceramic discs, splattering water around the filter basin.

By breaking the water into tiny droplets, the system maximises the water's contact with air, helping to increase oxygen levels and oxidise iron in the water (common in this area), before being further filtered in the basin below.
