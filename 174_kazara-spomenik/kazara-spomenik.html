Monument to the Revolution

Kozara National Park, Bosnia and Herzegovina

The Kozara Offensive was a major Axis operation targeting Partisan forces in the mountainous region of Kozara, in what is now Bosnia and Herzegovina. It became a cornerstone of national resistance mythology in post-war Yugoslavia, as Partisan leader Josip Tito and a few hundred fighters managed a narrow escape just as tens of thousands of enemy soldiers closed in.

“Kozara has survived one of the most difficult and one of the most famous epics in the history of our people at the same time. In the battle at Kozara many victims have fallen for what we have today. It was the beginning of the greatest struggle for brotherhood and unity in our country.”
– Josip Tito

Construction of a monument commemorating the suffering and sacrifice of this battle began in 1969, based on the winning design by Dušan Džamonja. The tall, cylindrical concrete structure stands in a clearing at the summit of Mrakovica, one of the highest peaks in the Kozara mountains.

As you approach the base of the 33-metre monolith, the contrasting details within its 20 concrete fins become more apparent.

Smooth and rough... ranging from embedded steel plates to sculpted and chipped concrete. Light and dark... shifting from the warm glow of the afternoon sun to the deep shadows of dark crevasses. Positive and negative... seen in the towering fins that rise above you, and the narrow gaps between them, just wide enough to slip through into the hollow centre of the spomenik.

Behind the spomenik, beyond the thick concrete fins, and at the edge of the forest, lies the memorial sanctum. A concrete-walled courtyard lined with bronze plaques.

Each plaque bears the names of hundreds of fighters and civilians who were killed during the Kozara Offensive. In total, 9,921 names are embossed.
